01 — the gap

Inadvertently projecting different emotions than you feel — or intend.

Without clear intonation melody, the emotional tone you project and the emotional tone you intend can be entirely different things. Users describe years of being misread — heard as cold, flat, aloof, or sarcastic — with no way to understand why, let alone fix it.

eric & elizabeth — how increased awareness and control of vocal tone lets them communicate emotional tenor effortlessly rather than fraught, and dramatically reduces the pressure to over-explain

— conversational flow

Enabling interruptions to be felt as contributions.

Redirection in conversation is natural — adding a thought, building on what someone said, shifting the thread. The problem isn't redirection itself. It's redirection at the wrong moment, when you can't feel where the speaker is in their arc. With Resonero, you feel the social dynamic moment-to-moment. What would otherwise land as an interruption lands as engagement. Conversations flow rather than fracture.

eric & elizabeth — conversational flow: how accurate turn-taking cues let them redirect naturally and non-disruptively. much like conversation in film, where close-miked audio delivers the same intonation information resonero brings into the room.

a portrait — a voice that was always there

One early user was a student with a genuinely warm and expressive voice. She could hear emotional nuance in recordings, in music, in film. But in person, across a table, none of that came through. She had been managing this for years: over-explaining, avoiding certain situations, working around something she couldn't name.

A conversation with Richard Posthuma — UTEP management faculty and negotiation researcher — illustrated the compensation-vs-signal distinction precisely. Richard described the normative pause duration skilled negotiators learn to track: a specific interval signalling when it's appropriate to respond. He trains people to consciously measure this.

Eric pointed out that this training is a workaround for missing signal. With Resonero in the room, you don't count the pause — you simply hear when someone is done. Richard found that reframe very interesting. We hope to record a conversation with him about this soon.

07 — the ambiance

Some users describe it as magic.

Several Resonero Home users have described the room ambiance the device creates as something like magic — a space that simply feels different, without being able to identify why. The device is invisible. Nothing visibly changed. The conversation just feels easier, warmer, more natural.

I don't know how to describe it. The room just feels different. Conversations feel easier. I kept looking around trying to figure out what changed.

early user — resonero home

My partner called it magic. I said it's physics. We were both right.

early user — resonero home

We think we know why. The emotional layer of conversation — reliably absent at social distances for most of human history — is present. That turns out to be noticeable, even when you can't name what changed.

08 — an unexpected finding

Sleep.

Several early users who left Resonero running in their bedrooms overnight reported something unexpected: urban noises that had previously triggered wakefulness — voices in the hall, sounds from outside — stopped doing so. They still heard the sounds. They just stopped being alarmed by them.

Our hypothesis: unidentified low-frequency sound triggers nervous system caution. Intonation Augmentation delivers intonation melody — enabling source identification. An identified source is not a threat. The nervous system stands down.

This wasn't what we were building for. We're paying attention to it.
