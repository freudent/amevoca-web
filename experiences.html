See yourself in others.

Real people describing something they didn't have a name for. These are their words.

These videos were recorded when the device was called Resonero — which is still its name. Some early videos also reference Social Resonance, an even earlier name. Re-labelled versions are on the way.

01 — the gap

Sounding different than you feel.

Within minutes of turning on Resonero, many users described a subtle shift — like the air surrounding them had softened, or like their thoughts had more space to breathe. Even after it's turned off, the feeling lingers. Several users reported feeling more grounded and present, and a decrease in anxious feelings or nervousness.

eric & elizabeth — increased awareness and control of vocal tone lets them communicate emotional tenor effortlessly rather than fraught, and dramatically reduces the pressure to over-explain

eric & elizabeth — how accurate turn-taking cues let them redirect conversations naturally without seeming rude. redirection is normal; doing it at the wrong moment isn't. with resonero, they feel the social dynamic moment-to-moment and conversations simply flow — fluid and non-disruptive, much like conversation in films, where close-miked audio delivers the same intonation information resonero brings into the room.

I'd watch videos of myself and think — that's not who I am. I'm not cold. I'm not flat. But that's what they were getting. I spent years trying to fix something I couldn't see clearly enough to fix.

early user — resonero home

People at work thought I was aloof. My family thought I was distracted. I was neither. I was trying harder than anyone in the room. I just had no way of knowing what was actually landing.

early user — resonero go

It wasn't any one conversation. It was the accumulation — years of relationships that never quite caught. I thought I was just difficult to connect with. That was wrong.

02 — intimate relationships

Partners, family, the people closest to you.

The highest-stakes conversations are the ones at home. When the person who knows you best still mishears your tone, or when you can't tell whether your child feels heard — that's where Resonero's impact is most felt.

My partner said I sound like myself now. I didn't realize I wasn't. But watching the recordings after — I can see exactly what she meant. Something was missing before.

early user — resonero home

He sounds like himself now. I can hear the care in his voice. He could always hear mine — just through a lot of interference. The interference is mostly gone.

03 — the cues you miss

Hearing only the words.

Small talk is a friendly emotional exchange — a way of saying "I see you, I'm glad you're here." The words are just carriers. Most people feel the emotional intent underneath. Those with unreliable intonation melody reconstruction hear only the words.

I used to respond literally to small talk. Someone would say "how was your weekend?" and I'd give them a real answer. They'd look at me strangely. I didn't understand why for a very long time.

early user — resonero go

I watched him get blindsided by a tone shift he completely missed. The other person's warmth had curdled twenty minutes earlier. He was still responding as if nothing had changed. It was painful to watch, because I knew he wasn't being oblivious — he genuinely couldn't hear it.

partner of early user
04 — at work

Meetings, presentations, negotiations.

Professional settings amplify the cost of misreading. Being heard as dismissive in a meeting, or missing the moment a negotiation shifted — these aren't small things.

Conversations at work stopped feeling like work. Something got easier that I didn't have a name for before. I stopped running the analysis in the background — the constant second-guessing of what people meant.

early user — resonero go

A conversation with Richard Posthuma — UTEP management faculty and negotiation researcher — illustrated this precisely. Richard described the normative pause duration that signals a speaker is done: a specific interval that skilled negotiators learn to recognize as the moment it's appropriate to respond. He trains people to consciously track this timing.

Eric pointed out that this training is compensation — a workaround for missing signal. With Resonero in the room, the nuances of conversational flow arrive naturally. You don't count the pause. You simply hear when someone is done. The dynamics work without effort, the way they do in close conversation, or in film. Richard found that reframe very interesting.

We hope to record a conversation with Richard about this soon.

05 — conversational dynamics

Turn-taking, interruption, cognitive load.

Conversation has a rhythm — knowing when someone is done, when they want a response, when to hold back. Without reliable intonation information, that rhythm is hard to feel. Users describe Resonero reducing the cognitive effort of tracking it.

08 — a portrait

A voice that was always there.

One early user was a student with a genuinely warm and expressive voice. She could hear emotional nuance in recordings, in music, in film. But in person, across a table, none of that came through — to her or to the people she was speaking with. She had been managing this for years: over-explaining, avoiding certain situations, working around something she couldn't name.

With Resonero in the room, I heard myself say something gentle — and it landed gently. For the first time. I didn't have to think about it or try to project it. It just went through.

09 — the ambiance

Some users describe it as magic.

Several Resonero Home users have described the room ambiance the device creates as something like magic — a space that simply feels different, without being able to identify why. The device is invisible. Nothing visibly changed. The conversation just feels easier, warmer, more natural.

I don't know how to describe it. The room just feels different. Conversations feel easier. I kept looking around trying to figure out what changed.

early user — resonero home

My partner called it magic. I said it's physics. We were both right.

early user — resonero home

We think we know why. The emotional layer of conversation — reliably absent at social distances for most of human history — is present. That turns out to be noticeable, even when you can't name what changed.

10 — an unexpected finding

Sleep.

Several early users who left Resonero running in their bedrooms overnight reported something unexpected: urban noises that had previously triggered wakefulness — voices in the hall, sounds from outside — stopped doing so. They still heard the sounds. They just stopped being alarmed by them.

Our hypothesis: unidentified low-frequency sound triggers nervous system caution. Intonation Augmentation delivers intonation melody — enabling source identification. An identified source is not a threat. The nervous system stands down.

This wasn't what we were building for. We're paying attention to it.
