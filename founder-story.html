How it started

A broken voice. A tool to fix singing. An unexpected discovery about everything else.

The problem I knew about

I knew my voice was broken. Not from theory — from recordings and from feedback.

When I sang: always wrong notes. When I spoke, recordings were always distressing. I discovered I actually sounded whiny. Like the Poindexter stereotype — nasal, pedantic, condescending. Nothing like how I felt internally. People told me I was too loud. I kept rudely interrupting when I was trying to be considerate. I had no idea why any of this was happening.

The weird thing was that my voice sounded so different as I spoke or sang. To me, in real-time, I sounded on-pitch when singing. Friendly when speaking. My internal experience and the external reality had zero connection.

Knowpitch (the predecessor)

I built a tool to solve the singing problem. A simple headphone-based system — you put them on and you immediately heard your actual intonation pitch as you produced it. No interface, no modes, no explanation. Just direct acoustic awareness.

The moment I put on Knowpitch, before I even started singing, people noticed. Someone asked: "What did you do with Eric? He looks like you but he's pushy, and you're not."

I wasn't trying to change how I spoke. The tool was only supposed to help me sing. But the effect was immediate and undeniable. Just having acoustic awareness of my intonation pitch changed how I came across to everyone.

Eventually my students wouldn't let me into the lab without Knowpitch. They weren't being cruel — they wanted me to be present as myself, the person they knew when I was wearing it. Without it, I was a different person to them. I didn't realize it until they pointed it out.

The insight

That's when I understood: intonation pitch wasn't just about singing. It was about the emotional layer of all speech. When my brain finally had accurate feedback about my intonation pitch, it affected how I sounded in every context — not just music.

But there was more. I also realized I had a listening problem. Without clear intonation pitch from others, I couldn't read conversational nuance. I'd try to be considerate, waiting for someone to finish before speaking. But I'd misjudge the cues. I'd jump in at what felt like the right moment — but people would say I was rudely interrupting. And I was always too loud. People would shush me constantly. I had no idea. The feedback I was getting about my own volume was as distorted as everything else.

The problem worked both ways: I wasn't broadcasting emotional content clearly, and I wasn't receiving it clearly either. My brain's reconstruction of others' intonation pitch was as distorted as theirs was of mine.

And I realized: this gap between internal perception and external reality — this wasn't unique to me. Other people described the exact same thing. They'd watch themselves on video and not recognize their own emotional content. People would take their words the wrong way. They'd feel warm inside but come across as cold. And they'd struggle to read when it was appropriate to speak, missing conversational cues that others perceived automatically.

I learned something interesting: some people who can sing have the same speaking voice challenges I do. One of my students was like this — she could sing beautifully with a rich chest voice, but in speaking she was loud, enthusiastic, interrupting when she meant to be thoughtful. I realized: she had the same quirky intonation perception that I did. Thanks to her chest voice, her intonation was prominent when singing, so she could hear it correctly and control it. But in speaking, her intonation wasn't prominent in her voice. She couldn't perceive it clearly. So she mis-perceived the emotional content of her own words — just like me when speaking. With Amevoca, her speaking voice finally made her intonation prominent enough to perceive, and suddenly she could express what she'd always felt — calm, caring, intentional — the person everyone knew her to be in singing.

Most people assume everyone perceives emotional content in speech the same way. But the gap in my own experience proved: that assumption was wrong.

The design matured

Five years of tinkering solved the big problem: how to get the same acoustic effect without headphones. And how to make it subtle enough that people don't notice it's there. The effect is so seamless that the director of the university's theater program commented that my office has the best acoustics of any space on campus. Turn it on and the room simply sounds warmer. The emotional content of speech becomes clear, to both speaker and listener.

We settled on two form factors that respect this invisibility: Amevoca Home, a bookshelf-size processor paired with a proper speaker for stationary use. And Amevoca Go, compact enough to travel, powered by a USB bank, fitting in a bag. There's almost nothing to see. Its sonic and visual invisibility are among Amevoca's best features. It disappears into the room. You don't notice any technical change in how voices sound. You just notice that conversations are a lot less effortful. And frustrating misunderstandings don't happen nearly so often.

The people who've used it for two years don't want to give it back.

Now

We're ready to share this with more people. No investors. No forced growth. Just careful work, funded by people who believe in it.

The first production run is weeks away.
