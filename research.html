pre-order
— resonero / research

We don't have peer-reviewed
validation yet. Here's what we do have.

Two years of consistent user reports. A published signals paper. An IRB-approved perception study underway. And a commitment to open data. We're building the evidence base — and we'd like your help.

01 — what we know and don't know

The mechanism is established. The outcomes evidence is not yet rigorous.

The Missing Fundamental Illusion is not in dispute — it is well-documented in the psychoacoustics literature. The differential attenuation of H1 (fundamental frequency, F0) relative to higher harmonics at social distances has been measured. The argument that this attenuation renders H1 inaudible to deficient MFI perceivers follows from established masking models.

What lacks rigorous peer-reviewed validation is the claim that Intonation Augmentation produces measurable improvements in communication outcomes under controlled conditions. Two years of consistent user reports give us confidence. That is not the same as a randomized controlled trial, and we won't pretend otherwise.

The research program below is how we're building that evidence.

02 — published work

Asilomar 2025: measuring H1 attenuation at social distances.

Our first published paper measured the actual attenuation of H1 relative to higher-order harmonics at socially normative conversational distances. We then argued — drawing on established masking models in the literature — that this attenuation would render H1 inaudible to perceivers with deficient MFI reconstruction.

Pre-research informal trials conducted in our lab in June 2025 observed that H1 delivered at −20dB relative to the harmonic series (without delay) was insufficient for deficient MFI perceivers to accurately perceive F0. This is consistent with the masking argument the Asilomar paper made from first principles. These are preliminary, pre-IRB observations — not controlled experiments — and should be read as such.

K. Moreno, N. Rovelli, C. Lauter and E. Freudenthal, "Intonation: Barely audible sounds that normies hallucinate," 2025 59th Asilomar Conference on Signals, Systems, and Computers, Pacific Grove, CA, USA, 2025, pp. 290–293.
doi: 10.1109/IEEECONF67917.2025.11443681

A note on the delay mechanism. Resonero re-inserts F0 with a slight delay relative to the direct voice signal. The delay makes the IA-delivered F0 perceptually separable from the ambient harmonic series — allowing effective delivery at much lower amplitude than the direct voice. This keeps Resonero acoustically unobtrusive and breaks the feedback loop that would otherwise occur at higher delivery levels.

03 — active and planned research

Three thrusts. One IRB-approved. Two in preparation.

All research is conducted under UTEP IRB oversight. Studies are approved before data collection begins. Most or all data will be made publicly available.

01

F0 perception — online crowdsourced IRB approved

An online psychoacoustic study examining how reliably participants perceive F0 from harmonic series under varying conditions. Crowdsourced, anonymous, conducted at scale. This is the scientific infrastructure behind the Resonero listening test — the population-level data it generates will characterize the distribution of MFI reconstruction reliability and help identify who benefits most from Intonation Augmentation.

Status: IRB approved. Active. Participate via the listening test →

02

Impact of IA on dyadic negotiation outcomes IRB in preparation

A controlled study examining whether Intonation Augmentation improves outcomes in dyadic negotiation — including physiological stress measures (HRV, pulse) alongside communication outcomes. Conducted in collaboration with Richard Posthuma (UTEP College of Business, management faculty). IRB filing in preparation. Data collection has not begun.

Status: IRB filing in preparation.

03

Therapeutic applications IRB in preparation

An exploration of possible therapeutic applications of Intonation Augmentation, in collaboration with mental health professionals. The specific research questions are being developed in partnership with clinical collaborators. IRB filing in preparation. Data collection has not begun.

Status: IRB filing in preparation.

04 — open data

We intend to make the data public.

Our intention is to make most or all research data publicly available following publication. We believe the questions Resonero raises — about intonation perception, MFI reconstruction variability, and the communicative consequences of acoustic attenuation — are larger than any one lab. The data should be accessible to anyone who wants to work with it.

If you have views on data format, repository, or licensing that would make this more useful for your work, we'd like to hear them.

06 — open empirical questions

What the recordings could tell us.

The three-era framework in the working paper above is grounded in acoustic physics and hardware history. What it cannot establish — because no recordings exist from before electrical amplification, and early recordings are sparse and uneven — is how speakers actually sounded and adapted across conditions. Two empirical questions remain open:

01

The PA consistency question

Pre-1950s political and ministerial recordings exist in both amplified and unamplified conditions for some speakers. If delivery is consistent across those conditions — same oratorical style regardless of amplification — it would suggest conventions were shaped by training and cultural expectation, not by acoustic feedback from the system. A null result here is publishable and informative. Audio and visual intimacy also shifted together — close-mic and close-up as parallel dimensions of the same technological move — making clean separation of causes difficult but not impossible.

02

The voice training question

How did elocution and vocal pedagogy change across the three eras? If training conventions shifted in ways that track H1 availability and hardware constraints, that's meaningful signal. Elocution manuals, conservatory records, and broadcast training guides from each era are likely accessible. We don't know what they say on this specific question.

These are empirical questions that people with archival access and relevant disciplinary training could address. If this is your territory — theatrical history, broadcast history, vocal pedagogy, acoustic phonetics, media history — we'd genuinely welcome contact. The working paper (section 07 below) provides the theoretical framework; these questions are what would test it.

07 — working paper

The Evolution of Oration.

Eric Freudenthal (with assistance from Gemini and Claude) · July 2026 · Unpublished

This paper proposes a three-era framework for understanding how sound reinforcement technology transformed public oratory — and argues that the conversational intimacy we now associate with authentic, credible speech is not a recovered historical baseline but a novel technological artifact of mid-20th century high-fidelity audio.

A widely held romantic narrative — associated with media theorists including Marshall McLuhan — holds that broadcast media recovered a kind of intimate, tribal vocal communication that characterized pre-industrial human communities. This paper argues the opposite. We don't know how prehistoric people actually spoke — no recordings exist, and the acoustic evidence is indirect. But the physics of unamplified speech in crowds, tents, and large spaces strongly suggests that declamatory projection, not conversational intimacy, was the dominant mode of public address for most of human history. Early electrical amplification, far from liberating speakers, transposed those same acoustic constraints into the electronic domain. What feels like recovered intimacy appears to be an entirely novel, synthetic phenomenon — an engineered asymmetric illusion of mass closeness that human technology produced for the first time in the mid-20th century. The ancient intimate voice may never have existed at scale. It may have been invented.

The three eras: Pre-Reinforcement — biological and architectural constraints forced declamatory projection, with speakers adapting physiology to survive outdoor crowds, tent acoustics, and cave reverberation. Early Reinforcement — carbon-granule microphones and primitive amplifiers transposed those same constraints into the electrical domain, locking the shouting style in place for two extra decades. Late Reinforcement — ribbon and dynamic microphones, close-miking, and full-spectrum reproduction liberated speakers from acoustic physics for the first time, transforming micro-tonal vocal subtlety from a liability into the dominant tool of mass persuasion. FDR's Fireside Chats are the historical marker of this transition.

The paper also examines the economic friction that slowed the transition — including the role of cheap crystal microphones as a compromise that extended the amplified-shout era into the 1950s across schools and churches — and a speculative extension into whether pre-reinforcement declamatory training, being rule-governed and rehearsed rather than spontaneous, may have been comparatively more accessible to speakers who rely on scripted language strategies.

The paper closes with a broader implication. If conversational intimacy at scale is a technological artifact of the mid-20th century, then so is the social expectation that everyone hears and produces it naturally. People whose auditory pathways engage in spectral rather than fundamental listening — hearing voice as it physically arrives rather than reconstructing a phantom fundamental — may be failing to meet a norm that is, in historical terms, extraordinarily recent. The social bias or stigma that attaches to people who seem emotionally flat, hard to read, or tonally off may reflect not a failure of ancient human capacity but a mismatch with a standard that has existed for barely one human lifetime. Resonero is, among other things, a response to that mismatch.

The paper is currently being shared with colleagues in acoustic history, media history, rhetorical studies, vocal pedagogy, and autism research for comment and potential collaboration. It is available on request. If you work in any of these fields and would like to read or respond to it, write to efreudenthal@utep.edu.
