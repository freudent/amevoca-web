pre-order
— resonero / research

We don't have peer-reviewed
validation yet. Here's what we do have.

Two years of consistent user reports. A published signals paper. An IRB-approved perception study underway. And a commitment to open data. We're building the evidence base — and we'd like your help.

01 — what we know and don't know

The mechanism is established. The outcomes evidence is not yet rigorous.

The Missing Fundamental Illusion is not in dispute — it is well-documented in the psychoacoustics literature. The differential attenuation of H1 (fundamental frequency, F0) relative to higher harmonics at social distances has been measured. The argument that this attenuation renders H1 inaudible to deficient MFI perceivers follows from established masking models.

What lacks rigorous peer-reviewed validation is the claim that Intonation Augmentation produces measurable improvements in communication outcomes under controlled conditions. Two years of consistent user reports give us confidence. That is not the same as a randomized controlled trial, and we won't pretend otherwise.

The research program below is how we're building that evidence.

02 — published work

Asilomar 2025: measuring H1 attenuation at social distances.

Our first published paper measured the actual attenuation of H1 relative to higher-order harmonics at socially normative conversational distances. We then argued — drawing on established masking models in the literature — that this attenuation would render H1 inaudible to perceivers with deficient MFI reconstruction.

Pre-research informal trials conducted in our lab in June 2025 observed that H1 delivered at −20dB relative to the harmonic series (without delay) was insufficient for deficient MFI perceivers to accurately perceive F0. This is consistent with the masking argument the Asilomar paper made from first principles. These are preliminary, pre-IRB observations — not controlled experiments — and should be read as such.

K. Moreno, N. Rovelli, C. Lauter and E. Freudenthal, "Intonation: Barely audible sounds that normies hallucinate," 2025 59th Asilomar Conference on Signals, Systems, and Computers, Pacific Grove, CA, USA, 2025, pp. 290–293.
doi: 10.1109/IEEECONF67917.2025.11443681

A note on the delay mechanism. Resonero re-inserts F0 with a slight delay relative to the direct voice signal. The delay makes the IA-delivered F0 perceptually separable from the ambient harmonic series — allowing effective delivery at much lower amplitude than the direct voice. This keeps Resonero acoustically unobtrusive and breaks the feedback loop that would otherwise occur at higher delivery levels.

03 — active and planned research

Three thrusts. One IRB-approved. Two in preparation.

All research is conducted under UTEP IRB oversight. Studies are approved before data collection begins. Most or all data will be made publicly available.

01

F0 perception — online crowdsourced IRB approved

An online psychoacoustic study examining how reliably participants perceive F0 from harmonic series under varying conditions. Crowdsourced, anonymous, conducted at scale. This is the scientific infrastructure behind the Resonero listening test — the population-level data it generates will characterize the distribution of MFI reconstruction reliability and help identify who benefits most from Intonation Augmentation.

Status: IRB approved. Active. Participate via the listening test →

02

Impact of IA on dyadic negotiation outcomes IRB in preparation

A controlled study examining whether Intonation Augmentation improves outcomes in dyadic negotiation — including physiological stress measures (HRV, pulse) alongside communication outcomes. Conducted in collaboration with Richard Posthuma (UTEP College of Business, management faculty). IRB filing in preparation. Data collection has not begun.

Status: IRB filing in preparation.

03

Therapeutic applications IRB in preparation

An exploration of possible therapeutic applications of Intonation Augmentation, in collaboration with mental health professionals. The specific research questions are being developed in partnership with clinical collaborators. IRB filing in preparation. Data collection has not begun.

Status: IRB filing in preparation.

04 — open data

We intend to make the data public.

Our intention is to make most or all research data publicly available following publication. We believe the questions Resonero raises — about intonation perception, MFI reconstruction variability, and the communicative consequences of acoustic attenuation — are larger than any one lab. The data should be accessible to anyone who wants to work with it.

If you have views on data format, repository, or licensing that would make this more useful for your work, we'd like to hear them.

06 — open questions we don't have time to pursue

Oratorical tradition, broadcast history, and the intimacy shift.

A conjecture from Eric, offered for scrutiny: speakers trained in pre-HiFi oratorical conventions brought those conventions unchanged into early PA contexts — because early PA systems were narrow-band enough that the acoustic experience for listeners wasn't qualitatively different from unamplified projection. The same frequencies that carried without PA carried with it. No new information channel opened; no new calibration incentive existed.

The incentive shift came later, when close-miking combined with full-spectrum reproduction created a genuinely new situation: a speaker could be heard at social distance with H1 intact, for the first time in any public speech context. That opened a channel that oratorical style wasn't using. Naturalistic delivery — previously inaudible at distance — became viable. The incentive to project emotional information through higher harmonics diminished because H1 was now doing work it had never done in public speech before.

Crucially, audio and visual intimacy shifted together. Close-mic and close-up arrived as parallel dimensions of the same technological move — collapsing the performer-audience distance that oratorical style was built around. They are not competing explanations; they are two channels of the same phenomenon.

Pre-1950s political and ministerial recordings offer a potentially tractable natural experiment. If speakers sound consistent across PA and non-PA conditions — using the same oratorical style regardless of amplification — that would suggest delivery conventions were not being calibrated to the acoustic chain. The contemporary credibility gap (why pre-HiFi delivery sounds mannered to us now) would then be explained primarily by our recalibration through decades of H1-carrying media, not by anything speakers were doing differently across conditions. A null result here is publishable and informative.

A more tractable sub-question: how has voice training changed? What did elocution and vocal pedagogy teach before electrical amplification, how did that change with the microphone, and how did it change again with full-spectrum close-mic reproduction? If training conventions shifted in ways that track H1 availability in the acoustic chain, that would be meaningful signal. We don't know whether they did.

This conjecture is offered as a hypothesis, not a finding. The confounds — oratorical tradition, class markers, cultural authority conventions, visual performance norms — are substantial, and the causal claims cannot be established without acoustic analysis of available recordings. We'd genuinely welcome collaboration from people with deep knowledge of theatrical history, broadcast history, vocal pedagogy, or acoustic phonetics. These questions sit at an intersection we're not equipped to work at ourselves, and we won't have time to pursue them soon. If this is your territory, please reach out.
