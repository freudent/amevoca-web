Why it works

The physics of intonation pitch, how your brain reconstructs it, and why Amevoca addresses what others don't.

The problem with how we hear

The emotional layer of speech — intonation pitch — is physically absent at normal social distances. It's not weakened or subtle. It's effectively gone. Your brain fills in what's missing, automatically and invisibly.

For most people, this reconstruction is accurate. They feel warmth as warmth, anger as anger, sadness as sadness. But for a significant number of people, that reconstruction is distorted. Not absent — distorted. Their brain produces a confident reading of the emotional content that may bear little relationship to what was intended.

The physics

A human voice is one of the most complex sounds in nature. What sounds like a single note is actually a rich cascade of frequencies: the intonation pitch (the lowest, most fundamental component) accompanied by a shimmering series of harmonics, each one a multiple of the first.

These components don't all travel equally. Higher frequencies, with their shorter wavelengths, can be directed by an aperture as small as a mouth — they beam forward toward the listener. But the intonation pitch, with its much longer wavelength, cannot be aimed the same way. It propagates spherically and dissipates so quickly that beyond about a meter, it's effectively gone.

Intonation pitch is only prominent at distances closer than social norms permit between anyone who isn't a lover or a young child held close.

For colleagues, friends, acquaintances, the people we spend most of our waking lives talking to — intonation pitch has always been absent from the signal.

How the brain reconstructs it

Whatever emotional meaning a listener perceives in normal social conversation is not received directly. It is reconstructed — by the brain, from the harmonics that did arrive, automatically and invisibly. This process is called the Missing Fundamental Illusion (MFI).

For people with accurate reconstruction, this works remarkably well. The perceived intonation closely matches what was intended. The emotional nuance lands.

For people with less reliable reconstruction, the reconstruction is distorted. They don't experience silence where intonation pitch was. They experience something else — a different mood, a different intent, a confident misreading that feels, to them, exactly like the truth.

An important note: Sometimes the problem isn't the person's perception — it's the acoustic information available. A student who struggled with communication in speaking (loud, interrupting, seeming pushy) could sing beautifully, because her chest voice naturally produced rich harmonics that made her intonation easier to reconstruct. Her intonation pitch was equally clear in both contexts, but listeners could only perceive it reliably when the harmonic structure supported reconstruction. This shows that intonation clarity depends not just on perception, but on the acoustic environment. Amevoca addresses this directly: it makes intonation audible regardless of harmonic structure.

Why speakers are also affected

The same physics applies to how speakers hear their own voices. The intonation pitch doesn't travel to your own ears reliably either. Without clear intonation pitch feedback, modulating your intonation is like trying to tune an instrument through a funhouse mirror.

Your intentions are genuine. The feedback that would let you act on them accurately simply isn't arriving.

The broadcast discovery

Almost everyone believes they hear others' intonation accurately. And in mediated contexts — television, radio, film, recorded music — they may well be right. Close-micing delivers intonation pitch intact.

Hi-fi reproduction extended to low frequencies in the 1960s, normalizing the experience of full-spectrum voice for entire populations. The broadcast industry discovered empirically that close-miced voices — with full intonation pitch intact — produce calm, trust, and warmth. Not stress. The classic broadcast voice isn't a technique. It's what a voice sounds like when intonation pitch actually arrives.

For people with less reliable reconstruction, emotional nuance in media works perfectly well. It only fails in real life, at social distances — quietly, invisibly, and without anyone knowing why.

How Amevoca addresses it

Amevoca (from Latin ame = love, voca = voice) implements Intonation Augmentation (IA) — an acoustic process that delivers the intonation pitch that physics removed. When you're hearing your own voice, IA provides clarifying feedback: you can finally hear what you're producing and shape it as you intend. When you're hearing others, IA clarifies what arrives: their emotional content reaches you directly, accurately, without requiring your brain to reconstruct it from incomplete information.

The result is simple: both speaker and listener have what they need. Your care, finally heard.
